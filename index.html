03

Empathize

Research Approach

We adopted a mixed-method approach to understand the nuanced reality of late-night commutes.

  • Primary: 5 In-depth Interviews with shift workers.
  • Secondary: Urban planning safety reports & crime statistics.

Research Questions

  • "How do shift workers currently decide which route to take home?"
  • "What psychological factors prevent users from reporting safety incidents?"
  • "Does seeing crime data make users feel safer or more anxious?"

Research Insights

01

Predictability creates safety

Users feel safest when information is predictable, structured, and easy to understand.

  • Uncertainty increases anxiety.
  • Route familiarity reduces stress.
02

Emotional impact matters

Alert systems must inform without overwhelming users.

  • Avoid 'fear scrolling'.
  • Focus on actionable info.
03

Trust through transparency

Trust is built when information is transparent, timely, and comes from credible sources.

  • Source verification is key.
  • Timestamps build confidence.

Competitor Analysis

Citizen

Real-time incident alerts with user-generated footage. Citizen delivers immediate notifications about nearby incidents but often uses sensational language and unverified reports. This can heighten anxiety and contribute to alert fatigue, making information feel overwhelming rather than helpful.

Gap: Induces anxiety ("fear scrolling") and lacks resolution for minor issues.

Neighbors by Ring

Safety updates sourced from local residents + device network footage. Neighbors emphasizes community-driven reporting, but information can feel inconsistent and lacks context about verification. The platform relies heavily on user participation, which can introduce bias and uneven coverage across different areas.

Gap: Focused on private property, less useful for public transit commuters.

Design Goal

Design a trusted safety experience by simplifying access to verified information, delivering calm and controlled alerts, and improving decision confidence during nightly commutes.

User Personas

The Vigilant Commuter

Wants information that can proactively notify her about safety issues and plan her route

Reflection

When I started Lumi, I thought I was designing an incident-reporting app. But through research and iteration, I realized I was designing something deeper: trust in vulnerable moments. In safety contexts, users don‚Äôt only judge usability ‚Äî they judge whether the experience feels safe, respectful, and credible. This project shifted my mindset from designing ‚Äúfeatures‚Äù to designing emotional confidence through privacy, tone, and clarity.

What I learned

  • Trust is the real product. If users don‚Äôt trust the platform, they won‚Äôt engage ‚Äî no matter how polished the UI is.
  • Safety UX is emotion-driven. In high-stress situations, people skim, hesitate, and avoid risk. Lumi taught me to design for limited attention, uncertainty, and fear, not ideal user behavior.
  • Microcopy drives action. Small wording decisions (prompts, privacy explanations, confirmation feedback) can determine whether users feel empowered to report or choose to stay silent.
  • Privacy is a core UX feature. Anonymity and user control aren‚Äôt ‚Äúsettings‚Äù. They‚Äôre essential design decisions that influence participation.

What I‚Äôd improve next

Because Lumi was a school project, I couldn‚Äôt validate decisions at scale. If this were a live product, I would:

  • Run A/B tests on reporting flow + microcopy to reduce drop-off and increase completion.
  • Track metrics like time-to-report, completion rate, repeat usage, and perceived trust.
  • Test credibility mechanisms (verification signals / moderation workflows) that protect community safety without discouraging participation.

Personal Growth

Lumi helped me move beyond designing screens to designing responsible, real-world experiences. I became more confident working with sensitive contexts, edge cases, and trade-offs, and learned how to design interactions that feel calm, clear, and human even when the situation isn‚Äôt.

This project reflects how I approach UX: balancing empathy, clarity, and real-world constraints, while designing for trust at the moments it matters most.

The Challenge

"Most staging courses focus on business setup or inspiration. The client needed a practical, step-by-step course that teaches staging execution and builds learner confidence. Because the course was still being recorded, we also needed a pre-launch funnel to grow an audience and sustain interest until launch."

Primary Users

  • Aspiring Home Stagers: Building a repeatable workflow.
  • Real Estate Agents: Wanting fluency to support sellers.
  • DIY Homeowners: Staging their own homes to sell.

Process (How I worked)

With the course still in production, I focused on making expert staging knowledge teachable and scalable:

  • Translated the instructor‚Äôs real-world staging workflow into a structured learning path
  • Identified high-friction moments for beginners and embedded actionable tools into each module

03

Key Decisions

Because the course was still being recorded, my priority was to design a system that could scale: a learner experience that is structured, actionable, and consistent across materials, plus a pre-launch journey that keeps audiences engaged until launch.

Decision 01: Structure the course as a repeatable workflow

What I did

Sequenced modules to mirror how staging work happens in real life (consultation ‚Üí buyer profile ‚Üí staging plan ‚Üí execution ‚Üí final walkthrough).

Why it mattered

Beginners struggle most when they have to decide ‚Äúwhat to do next.‚Äù A workflow-based sequence reduces uncertainty and gives learners a clear mental model of the staging process.

Impact on design

Each module ends with actionable outputs (checklists, prompts, decision rules) so learners can apply the workflow immediately.

Decision 02: Design one reusable learning system (not 10 separate modules)

What I did

Created a consistent content system across modules: repeated slide patterns, workbook templates, ‚Äúpro tip‚Äù blocks, checklists, and framework layouts.

Why it mattered

Changing layouts every module increases cognitive load. A repeatable system lets learners focus on staging execution rather than interpreting how to use the materials.

Impact on design

The slide deck and workbook share the same structure so learning feels cohesive and predictable.

Decision 03: Treat the pre-launch funnel as part of the service experience

What I did

Designed a pre-launch waitlist journey that captures interest, delivers immediate value, and builds trust over time through structured communication.

Why it mattered

Because the course was not live yet, the funnel needed to do more than collect emails. It had to create momentum and reduce drop-off between sign-up and launch.

Impact on design

This shaped the funnel into a service journey:

  • Immediate reward (lead magnet)
  • Ongoing updates (nurture)
  • Launch countdown (conversion moment)

With the course still in production, impact was measured through launch readiness and system scalability.

  • A structured learning journey that reduces uncertainty for beginners
  • A reusable slide + workbook system that scales across modules without increasing cognitive load
  • A pre-launch waitlist experience that captures demand and builds trust before launch
