Lumi

A reporting + alert service concept to reduce anxiety for late-night shift workers by filtering safe zones and enabling fast reporting.

Problem Statement

"Late-night shift workers often feel unsafe commuting home alone. Existing tools are too slow, too complex, or not designed for proactive support — leaving users without real-time reassurance and structured reporting when it matters."

The Challenge

Design a service that provides safety and reassurance during high-stress moments while minimizing cognitive load. The solution needed to balance privacy, credibility, and real-world feasibility for integration with city transit systems.

Business Challenges

Service Implementation

Bridging the gap between user reporting and official city data without overwhelming infrastructure maintenance teams.

Trust vs. Speed

Building a system that verifies reports instantly without human moderation to ensure scalability.

03

Empathize

Research Approach

We adopted a mixed-method approach to understand the nuanced reality of late-night commutes.

  • Primary: 5 In-depth Interviews with shift workers.
  • Secondary: Urban planning safety reports & crime statistics.

Research Questions

  • "How do shift workers currently decide which route to take home?"
  • "What psychological factors prevent users from reporting safety incidents?"
  • "Does seeing crime data make users feel safer or more anxious?"

Research Insights

01

Predictability creates safety

Users feel safest when information is predictable, structured, and easy to understand.

  • Uncertainty increases anxiety.
  • Route familiarity reduces stress.
02

Emotional impact matters

Alert systems must inform without overwhelming users.

  • Avoid 'fear scrolling'.
  • Focus on actionable info.
03

Trust through transparency

Trust is built when information is transparent, timely, and comes from credible sources.

  • Source verification is key.
  • Timestamps build confidence.

Competitor Analysis

Citizen

Real-time incident alerts with user-generated footage. Citizen delivers immediate notifications about nearby incidents but often uses sensational language and unverified reports. This can heighten anxiety and contribute to alert fatigue, making information feel overwhelming rather than helpful.

Gap: Induces anxiety ("fear scrolling") and lacks resolution for minor issues.

Neighbors by Ring

Safety updates sourced from local residents + device network footage. Neighbors emphasizes community-driven reporting, but information can feel inconsistent and lacks context about verification. The platform relies heavily on user participation, which can introduce bias and uneven coverage across different areas.

Gap: Focused on private property, less useful for public transit commuters.
04

Define

Design Goal

Design a trusted safety experience by simplifying access to verified information, delivering calm and controlled alerts, and improving decision confidence during nightly commutes.

User Personas

The Vigilant Commuter

Wants information that can proactively notify her about safety issues and plan her route

The Community Steward

Wants information that can keep her informed about the safety of her local area and her family.

09

Reflection

When I started Lumi, I thought I was designing an incident-reporting app. But through research and iteration, I realized I was designing something deeper: trust in vulnerable moments. In safety contexts, users don’t only judge usability — they judge whether the experience feels safe, respectful, and credible. This project shifted my mindset from designing “features” to designing emotional confidence through privacy, tone, and clarity.

What I learned

  • Trust is the real product. If users don’t trust the platform, they won’t engage — no matter how polished the UI is.
  • Safety UX is emotion-driven. In high-stress situations, people skim, hesitate, and avoid risk. Lumi taught me to design for limited attention, uncertainty, and fear, not ideal user behavior.
  • Microcopy drives action. Small wording decisions (prompts, privacy explanations, confirmation feedback) can determine whether users feel empowered to report or choose to stay silent.
  • Privacy is a core UX feature. Anonymity and user control aren’t “settings”. They’re essential design decisions that influence participation.

What I’d improve next

Because Lumi was a school project, I couldn’t validate decisions at scale. If this were a live product, I would:

  • Run A/B tests on reporting flow + microcopy to reduce drop-off and increase completion.
  • Track metrics like time-to-report, completion rate, repeat usage, and perceived trust.
  • Test credibility mechanisms (verification signals / moderation workflows) that protect community safety without discouraging participation.

Personal Growth

Lumi helped me move beyond designing screens to designing responsible, real-world experiences. I became more confident working with sensitive contexts, edge cases, and trade-offs, and learned how to design interactions that feel calm, clear, and human even when the situation isn’t.

This project reflects how I approach UX: balancing empathy, clarity, and real-world constraints, while designing for trust at the moments it matters most.

Staging Mastery

Designing an End-to-End Course Experience & Pre-Launch Funnel (Wix → Kajabi)

Overview Challenge & Users Process Design Decisions Solution Outcomes & Reflection

Project Snapshot

Role

Field Placement (End-to-End Design)

Tools

Wix, Kajabi, Canva, Google Workspace

Deliverables

Curriculum architecture (10 modules), workbook system, slide system, pre-launch funnel, email nurture plan

Status

Pre-launch (Course production in progress)

My Role & Responsibilities

I owned the end-to-end experience design across content + service touchpoints:

  • Curriculum architecture and module progression (10 modules)
  • Workbook system (checklists, exercises, applied frameworks)
  • Slide system (instruction + reinforcement)
  • Pre-launch waitlist funnel design (Wix now, Kajabi later)
  • Email nurture + promotion countdown plan
  • Supporting marketing materials for social and launch communications

The Challenge

"Most online staging courses emphasize business setup or general inspiration. The team needed a practical, step-by-step course that teaches staging execution—plus a launch-ready funnel to build an audience while the course was being recorded."

Primary Users

  • Aspiring Home Stagers: Building a repeatable workflow.
  • Real Estate Agents: Wanting fluency to support sellers.
  • DIY Homeowners: Staging their own homes to sell.

Shared Need: Confidence through a clear process and tools that reduce uncertainty.

01

Process & Key Decisions

1 Discover

With the course still in production, I focused discovery on making expert staging knowledge teachable and scalable:

  • Extracted the instructor’s real staging workflow and converted it into a structured learning path.
  • Identified high-friction moments for beginners (e.g., consultation conversations, buyer profiling, layout decisions) and ensured each module included actionable tools.
  • Planned the launch journey as a service experience—not just a website—so early interest could be captured and nurtured pre-launch.

2 Define

I established four core experience goals to guide the design:

Make the learning path obvious

Progression that mirrors a real staging workflow.

Make learning actionable

Checklists, prompts, and reusable frameworks.

Reduce cognitive load

Consistent templates across modules.

Enable launch readiness

Waitlist funnel now; automation later.

03

Design Decisions

Decision A — Sequence modules as a workflow, not a topic list

Instead of organizing content as disconnected tips, I structured modules to mirror real staging engagements (consultation → buyer mindset → staging plan → execution details → final walkthrough), so learners always know what to do next.

Decision B — Build a reusable content system

I designed repeatable layouts and patterns across modules (visual hierarchy, “pro tips,” checklists) so learners can focus on learning staging—not figuring out how to read the materials each time.

Decision C — Treat the funnel as part of the service experience

Because the course wasn’t live yet, the funnel needed to do two jobs:

  • Capture interest now and reward it immediately (lead magnet)
  • Keep subscribers engaged until launch (updates + eventual promotion countdown)

This shaped the funnel into a pre-launch waitlist journey that can later scale on Kajabi via popups, dedicated opt-in pages, and automation.

04

The Solution (What I Built)

A) Curriculum Architecture (10 Modules)

I created a 10-module path that builds from fundamentals to execution and client communication. The structure supports progressive skill-building and reduces overwhelm by guiding learners through a clear sequence.

B) Workbook System (Hands-on Learning)

The workbook converts passive learning into action through consultation checklists, prompts for buyer demographics, and practical rules learners can reuse.

Consultation Checklists

Visual Roadmaps

C) Slide System (Instruction + Reinforcement)

Slides were designed to align with workbook structure using clear hierarchy, chunked bullets, and consistent patterns.

D) Pre-Launch Waitlist Funnel

Current State (Wix)

Visitors discover Staging Mastery via the StagedSetSold website and land on a pre-launch page designed around a single conversion goal: waitlist sign-up.

Future State (Kajabi)

The funnel scales with popup opt-ins, dedicated sign-up pages, embedded forms, and automated email sequences.

Lead Magnet Strategy

Newsletter sign-ups receive a free Rug Placement Guide PDF as immediate value.

Followed by pre-launch updates and a promotion countdown sequence.

05

Outcomes & Reflection

Pre-Launch Deliverables

Because the course is still being recorded, outcomes are measured as launch readiness.

  • Completed 10-module curriculum architecture.
  • Created cohesive workbook & slide systems.
  • Established pre-launch waitlist funnel & scaling plan.

Measurement Plan

Once live, I would evaluate the experience using:

  • Landing page conversion (visit → subscribe).
  • Lead magnet engagement (delivery and click-through).
  • Enrollment conversion after promotion countdown.
  • Course completion rates.

Reflection & Next Steps

If continuing post-launch, I would:

Run a small pilot cohort A/B test landing page messaging Build dashboards for engagement Expand acquisition via paid social

Echoes from China

Project Snapshot

Role

Web Designer (UX), IA, Content Writer

Team

2-person team (Co-led design & build)

Tools

Wix, Canva, Instagram

Key Features

Event signup, Blog, Forum, Donation

Project Summary

Echoes from China is a traditional arts society dedicated to promoting Chinese culture and connecting people through cultural events and community learning. Since the organization had no official online presence, my teammate and I designed and built the website from scratch—creating a central platform to support event registration, membership growth, donations, and cultural storytelling.

Final homepage design presenting a clear organizational identity and primary call-to-action.

The Challenge

"Echoes from China did not have an official platform to introduce the organization, promote events, or support membership. Information existed across informal channels, making it difficult for potential members to understand what the society offers and how to join."

How might we design an official website that supports both event conversion and long-term community building?

Project Goals

  • Create an official website as a credible digital home.
  • Enable ticket sales and event registration.
  • Support the ability to accept donations.
  • Provide membership functionality to attract talent.
  • Build a culturally authentic website with strong aesthetics.

My Role

I led the end-to-end design and build of the website, including:

  • Website UX and visual design (Wix)
  • Information architecture + navigation structure
  • Content writing (About Us, offerings, events, blog structure)
  • Designing conversion flows for event signup + membership
  • Iterating based on stakeholder feedback under a tight timeline

Designing and building the website in Wix—from IA planning to final content and layout.

01

Research & Inspiration

Research (3 Interviews)

To ensure the website met real needs, we interviewed:

  • Co-founder: Organizational goals & priorities.
  • Club member: Motivations for participation.
  • Culture enthusiast: Expectations & trust factors.

Key Insights

  • 1) Events are the entry point. Most users engage through events first.
  • 2) Clarity is crucial. Visitors need to instantly understand what the org offers.
  • 3) Community requires continuity. Membership spaces support long-term engagement.

Benchmarking

I studied relevant websites (Toronto Chinese Orchestra, SiRu Plum) to guide layout decisions and maintain cultural aesthetics without compromising usability.

Benchmark websites informed the aesthetic direction.

02

IA & Design Decisions

Information Architecture

I designed the navigation around key user tasks to support both new visitors seeking clarity and returning members looking for continuity.

IA structured around user goals: discover → participate → engage long-term.

Key Features & Design

Event Signup

A conversion-centered event experience that supports ticketing and registration.

Membership / Forum

A community layer enabling long-term engagement and member interaction.

SEO & Discoverability

To support growth, I implemented SEO recommendations including custom URL structures, optimized page titles, and a keyword strategy focusing on "traditional Chinese culture" and "Toronto".

03

Outcome & Reflection

Deliverables

  • Launched Echoes from China’s first official website (0→1).
  • Established event registration/ticketing structure.
  • Built donation and membership functionality.
  • Implemented SEO growth foundations.

Reflection

This project strengthened my ability to:

  • Build a website from scratch under real constraints.
  • Translate stakeholder goals into IA and user journeys.
  • Design for both conversion and community retention.
  • Balance cultural storytelling with modern usability.
